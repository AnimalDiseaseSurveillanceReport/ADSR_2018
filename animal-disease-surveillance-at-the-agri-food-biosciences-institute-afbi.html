Animal Disease Surveillance





Section 17 Animal Disease Surveillance at the Agri-Food & Biosciences Institute (AFBI)

  • Siobhan Corry,
    • Research Officer, Omagh Regional Veterinary Laboratory, 43 Beltany Rd, Omagh BT78 5NF, Northern Ireland
  • Alice Walsh
    • University College Dublin, Stillorgan Rd, Belfield, Dublin, Ireland



AFBI provides animal disease surveillance and diagnostic services for the Northern Irish farming industry. Passive (scanning) surveillance is carried out by collecting and analysing data from post mortem and clinical samples submitted to the Omagh and Stormont laboratory sites of the Disease Surveillance and Investigation Branch (DSIB) of AFBI. Analysis of this data assists in establishing baselines for incidence of endemic disease, facilitating recognition of new and emerging diseases and allowing rapid identification of changing trends in disease prevalence. This allows evidence based decision making by veterinary providers and at national level.

17.1 Cattle Diseases

17.1.1 Neonatal Calves (0–1 months)

As in previous years, enteric infections were the most frequently diagnosed cause of death for this age group in 2018 in calves submitted to AFBI for post mortem (see Table 17.1 and Figure 17.1 and the breakdown on bovine neonatal enteritis in Table 17.9 and Figure 17.15.

Failure of passive transfer of maternal immunity was detected in 74 per cent of blood samples tested using ZST (zinc sulphate turbity test) from calves aged less than 10 days (Figure 17.16, highlighting the importance of good colostrum management. This year respiratory infections were the second most commonly diagnosed cause of death for this age group.

In 2018, respiratory infections accounted for just under half (46.6 per cent) of all deaths in this age group in calves submitted to AFBI for post mortem (Table 17.2 and Figure 17.3), highlighting the economic and welfare implications of respiratory disease (see also Table 17.6 and Figure 17.10 in the Bovine Respiratory Disease section below).

Bacterial causes of pneumonia were the most frequently diagnosed respiratory infections at AFBI in 2018. Mycoplasma bovis was the most frequently recorded pathogen and was detected in 29 per cent of respiratory infections, followed by Pasteurella multocida (14 per cent of respiratory infections). Mannheimia haemolytica was diagnosed in 7 per cent of respiratory infections and Trueperella pyogenes was isolated in 6 per cent of cases. Bovine respiratory syncytial virus (BRSV) was the most frequently diagnosed viral cause of infection in this age of calves and was detected in 9 per cent of cases and was thus the third most frequently recorded respiratory pathogen in 2018. Infectious bovine rhinotracheitis virus (IBRV) was detected in 1% of respiratory infections in this age group.

The importance of suitable housing, ventilation and control of stress factors in calves of this age are issues that need to be addressed in order for this situation to improve. Vaccination programmes and antibiotic care, where appropriate, should also be discussed.

Intestinal or gastrointestinal torsions accounted for 6 per cent of overall diagnoses in this age group. Enteric infections were the third most frequently diagnosed cause of death in calves from one to five months of age and included infections such as cryptosporidiosis, parasitic gastroenteritis, coccidiosis and coronaviral infections.

Clostridial disease was again important in this age group despite the availability of effective clostridial vaccines to help prevent losses. Blackleg was the most commonly recorded clostridial disease in one to five month old calves representing 56 per cent of the clostridial diseases recorded. Botulism accounted for 19 per cent of the clostridial diagnoses and malignant oedema made up 12 per cent of clostridial disease recorded. Over 70 per cent of poisoning diagnoses in this age group were due to lead intoxication.

17.1.3 Calves (6–12 months)

In 2018, with an incidence of 46.2 per cent of cases, respiratory infections were also the most commonly diagnosed cause of death in this age group (Table 17.3) and Figure 17.5), this is a slight decrease when compared to 2017, 49.1 per cent.

Bacterial infections were the most frequent cause of respiratory tract infections. Of the bacterial infections, Mycoplasma bovis was most often detected (15 cases representing 20 per cent of respiratory tract infections) followed by Pasteurella multocida (8 cases or 11per cent of respiratory infections), Mannheimia haemolytica (8 cases or 11per cent of respiratory infections), Haemophilus somni (4 cases or 5per cent of respiratory infections) and Arcanobacterium pyogenes (2 cases) . Four cases of bovine respiratory syncytial virus and one case of IBR were recorded. Waning passive immunity derived from colostrum feeding, autumn housing, weaning, mixing and transport as well as husbandry activities such as dehorning and castration increase stress and help predispose to outbreaks of viral and bacterial respiratory disease in this age group of calves.

Despite availability of cheap and effective vaccines, clostridial diseases were identified as the second largest cause of mortality in this age group at 17.5 per cent.

Blackleg occurred most commonly in this age range and was diagnosed in 21 cases, while 5 cases of botulism and two cases of black disease were diagnosed. For the first time since an all-island Animal Disease Surveillance Report was first produced by AFBI and DAFM in 2010, 2018 was the first year in which no cases of BVD or mucosal disease were detected in weanling cattle in the North of Ireland demonstrating the progress being made by the BVD eradication scheme (Figure 17.6).

17.2 Bovine Respiratory Disease (BRD)

Bovine respiratory disease was the most commonly identified cause of death in all age groups of bovines, other than neonatal calves, submitted to AFBI in 2018 and was the second most commonly identified cause of death in neonatal calves. There were multiple aetiologies identified (Table 17.5). The economic cost of wastage and reduced productivity due to cattle respiratory infections are considerable.

Table 17.5: Most common diagnostic groups on post-mortem examinations of bovine respiratory disease by AFBI during 2018 (n= 236 ).
Category No. of cases Percentage
Bacterial 184 78.0
Parasitic 31 13.1
Viral 21 8.9

As in 2017, in 2018 Mycoplasma bovis was the pathogen identified with the greatest frequency in cattle diagnosed with respiratory disease, increasing from 23.2 per cent to 30.9 per cent. At AFBI, M. bovis is detected by PCR tests, positive results need to be interpreted in conjunction with gross and histopathological findings.

Mycoplasma bovis adhere closely to host cells and facilitate damage to cells by soluble factors such as hydrogen peroxide, surface protein variation allows the organism to evade developing immune response by rapidly adapting to internal host environment. M. bovis can cause severe pneumonia in the absence of other organisms or can add to disease where the causative pathogens are Pasteurella spp. or Mannheimia spp. (Quinn et al. 2015). Good management practices and antimicrobial therapy are the most important methods of treatment and control. However, there may be a poor response if disease presentation is chronic.

M. bovis was followed in percentage by P. haemolytica, P. multocida, D. viviparus (lungworm), T. pyogenes, BRSV, H. somnus, IBR and BVD (Table 17.6 and Figure 17.10).

17.2.0.1 Lungworm

Parasitic bronchitis due to lungworm Dictyocaulus viviparus (Figure 17.9) was seen in 13.1 per cent of bovine respiratory disease cases, with a seasonal peak towards November (Figure 17.11). In areas with a mild climate, high levels of rainfall and abundance of permanent grassland, outbreaks of this disease can occur at any stage between June and November, most commonly from July until September.

Figure 17.10: Relative frequency of diagnoses in bovine respiratory disease recorded by AFBI during 2018 (n= 236 ).

Generally, only calves in their first grazing year are clinically affected, as older animals in endemic areas generally have strong acquired immunity. Infection may persist in calves through overwintered larvae on pasture (from autumn until late spring) and carrier animals with arrested larvae persisting in the bronchi until the next grazing season. Although dairy calves are more typically affected than beef calves, autumn-born sucklers have the same level of susceptibility once they are turned out in early summer.

Adult cattle can be infected with parasitic bronchitis if they have not had sufficient exposure in previous years to develop an adequate immune response prior to being grazed on pastures with a heavy burden of parasites.

Control can be achieved through a lungworm vaccination programme given orally to calves over 8 weeks of age and by the use of prophylactic early-season anthelmintic treatments or rumen boluses; though the latter should be considered with care so as not to deny treated animals exposure to natural challenge, which may result in susceptibility later in life.

Figure 17.11: Annual distribution of deaths in cattle due to lungworm recorded by AFBI during 2018 (n= 39 ).

17.3 Bovine Abortion

Bovine abortion is a significant cause of livestock wastage and results in considerable costs to the cattle industry. As a general guideline, once level of abortions exceeds 3 to 5 per cent or if there are several abortions clustered together, an investigation should be carried out. Specimens from 367 (Table 17.7 and Figure 17.12) bovine abortions and stillbirths were examined during 2018 (427 were examined in 2017).

Significant pathogens were detected in 153 of these, 41.7 per cent. This diagnostic rate is comparable to other years and reflects the multiple aetiologies of bovine abortion and the fact that not all abortions are due to an infectious cause.

As in previous years, Trueperella pyogenes, Salmonella Dublin and Bacillus licheniformis were the most commonly diagnosed pathogens. Neospora caninum was the fourth most commonly diagnosed pathogen followed by BVDV and Leptospirosis. S. Dublin presented a characteristic annual distribution with an increase at the end of the year with a peak in November (Figure 17.13).

T. pyogenes is considered a sporadic cause of abortion through haematogenous spread and placentitis. It is suspected that the organism penetrates into the bloodstream from mucosal surfaces and, when the animal is pregant, can result in placental localisation and abortion; this occurs primarily in the last trimester but, potentially, at any stage of gestation. Illness in dams usually follows this kind of abortion and may prove fatal due to mastitis, arthritis or suppurative endometritis.

Bacillus licheniformis also results in necrosuppurative placentitis but, unlike T. pyogenes, does not cause any signs of systemic illness in the cow. B. licheniformis is ubiquitous, although silage, run-off water, foodstuffs and bedding that have become contaminated with silage effluent and wet spoiled hay are the most likely sources of infection through suspected haematogenous entry from the gastrointestinal tract.

A total of 945 bacterial isolates were cultured from milk samples submitted from acute and chronic mastitis cases (Table 17.8 and Figure 17.14). Significance of the organism depends on cell count, organism isolated and whether or not this organism was isolated in pure culture. Isolation of 3 or more species suggests contamination during sampling. Result interpretation should, therefore, be undertaken with care.

E.coli was the most frequently isolated organism accounting for 21.2 per cent of isolates cultured, compared with 22.8 per cent in 2017. E.coli is the most prevalent environmental cause of mastitis and produces opportunistic infections in animals with reduced immune status or animals with heavy exposure to pathogenic strains of the bacteria. Infection primarily occurs when the teat canal is open. Risk factors include poor hygiene, suboptimal milking machine function, teat-end damage and lactation.

Another frequently identified environmental organism, Streptococcus uberis, is a naturally resident of skin, tonsillar tissue and vaginal mucosa and was identified in 16.3 per cent of submitted samples, a decline on previous years. Contamination usually occurs in housed animals due to poor environmental hygiene.

Staphylococcus aureus, a contagious cause of mastitis that typically spreads from cow to cow via milking equipment and contaminated milker’s hands, was identified in 6.6 per cent of samples submitted in 2018, compared to 8.5 per cent in 2017. Most S. aureus infections are subclinical, though they can vary from peracute life-threatening systemic forms to chronic infections which lead to elevated somatic cell counts, abscessation and fibrosis of collecting ducts. Chronic, low-grade and subclinical infections are a significant cause of economic losses through a drop in production and the withholding of milk during antibiotic treatment.

Approximately 850 neonatal faecal samples were examined by AFBI in 2018 (Table 17.9 and Figure 17.15). As in previous years, Cryptosporidium spp. was the most frequently identified pathogen, 34.4 per cent, Rotavirus was the second, 28.3 per cent, and Coronavirus the third, 8 per cent. Faecal samples sent to AFBI from calves younger than two weeks of age are also checked for E.coli expressing the K99 fimbrial antigen. E.coli K99 has been associated with enterotoxigenic colibacillosis in neonates. In 2018, E.coli K99 was identified in 2.7 per cent of faecal samples from calves younger than 2 weeks of age.

Neonatal calves are most susceptible to infection during the first few days of life with watery diarrhoea being the usual first clinical sign. The low pH of the distal small intestine provides the ideal growth environment for pathogens, which colonise the gut epithelium and cause villous atrophy and up-regulation of chloride secretion, pulling water into the lumen of the intestine by osmosis and resulting in the classic secretory diarrhoea (Malmuthuge and Guan 2017).

The Zinc Sulphate Turbidity test is an indirect measurement of the passive transfer of immunoglobulins from the dam to the neonate. The test involves precipitating high molecular weight proteins out of solution, which causes turbidity that is then assessed using a colourimeter. There is a clear correlation between low ZST results and poor health in calves, relating to both low IgG levels and low total immunoglobulin levels.

Figure 17.16: Results of Zinc Sulphate Turbidity tests performed by AFBI in 2018 from bovine calf serum samples taken at post-mortem (n= 188 ). Adequate colostral immunity is defined as equal or greater than to 20 units.

Figure 17.17: Results of Zinc Sulphate Turbidity tests performed by AFBI in 2018 from submitted diagnostic bovine calf serum samples (n= 968 ). Adequate colostral immunity is defined as equal or greater than 20 units.

Figure 17.18: Distribution and violin plot of results of ZST values in AFBI during 2018 from submitted diagnostic bovine calf serum samples. Adequate colostral immunity is defined as equal or greater than 20 units (orange line). Those cases greater than 60 units are not included.

During 2018, 1156 blood samples (submitted by veterinary practitioners or collected at post-mortem examination of carcases at AFBI) were tested by AFBI. Of these 694 (60.03 per cent) returned a value of lower than 20 units, i.e. there had been inadequate passive transfer of immunoglobulins.

Further analysis shows that, of the 968 blood samples submitted by vets in practice, 555 (57.33 per cent) were inadequate (Figure 17.17) and of the 188 samples taken during a post mortem 139 (73.94 per cent) were inadequate (Figure 17.16), highlighting the link between calf mortality and inadequate passive transfer of immunoglobulins. Distribution of results of ZST values from submitted diagnostic bovine calf serum samples is plotted in a violin plot in Figure 17.18.

Gut closure in neonates begins almost immediately after birth, consequently calves need to get an adequate volume of colostrum, at least three litres, within the first two hours of life. Calves will acquire 90 per cent of their immunoglobulins through colostrum, therefore, correct colostrum management is essential.

17.7.1 Liver fluke

Figure 17.21: Relative frequency of detection of liver fluke eggs in bovine faecal samples examined in AFBI in 2018 (n=2001).

In 2018, Fasciola hepatica incidence dropped to 12.5 per cent of bovine faecal samples submitted to AFBI (Figure 17.21). Pathogenesis depends on the number of ingested metacercariae (immature fluke) and stage of parasite development within the liver; first phase occurs while parasites migrate through hepatic parenchyma and the second while parasites reside in bile ducts, the latter leading to characteristic calcification of ducts and the pipe-stem liver appearance visible on post-mortem (Taylor, Coop, and Wall 2015). Diagnosis is most common in late winter and early spring. Liver fluke infection, Fasciolosis, has major economic associations on livestock productivity due to the resulting morbidity and mortality (McCann, Baylis, and Williams 2010), with carcases that have been infected by liver fluke having poorer conformation and lower cold weight than those free of liver fluke (Sanchez-Vazquez and Lewis 2013).

17.7.2 Rumen fluke

Figure 17.22: Relative frequency of detection of rumen fluke eggs in bovine faecal samples examined in AFBI in 2018 (n=1998).

Paramphistomum spp. are found in the reticulum and rumen and are generally well tolerated, even with heavy burdens. Any pathogenic effect is usually associated with the intestinal phase of infection, where immature flukes attach to duodenal mucosa before migrating to forestomachs; diarrhoea, anorexia and rectal haemorrhage may be noted. However, a large number of animals with rumen fluke eggs detected in their faeces show few, if indeed any, clinical signs of disease. Incidence of positive bovine faecal samples remains similar to 2017, 55 per cent (Figure 17.22).

17.7.3 Coccidia spp.

Figure 17.23: AFBI results for bovine faecal samples tested for coccidial oocysts during 2018 (n=2281).

Animals are infected by ingesting oocysts from contaminated pasture. Coccidiosis can cause significant economic losses to farmers due to reduced performance and mortality caused by heavy burden predominantly in younger animals. In 2018 there was a slight decrease in the percentage of positive samples, from 27 per cent in 2017 to 23 per centin 2018 (Figure 17.23).

Foetii from 333 ovine abortions and stillbirths were examined during 2018 in Northern Ireland (Table 17.12 and Figure 17.32), compared to 244 abortions examinations in 2017. Significant pathogens were detected in 210 cases (63 per cent). Pathogens identified included Toxoplasma gondii (74 cases, 22.2 per cent), Chlamydophilia abortus (39 cases, 11.7 per cent), E. coli (22 cases, 6.6 per cent), Campylobacter spp. (11 cases, 3.3 per cent), Salmonella spp. (10 cases, 3 per cent ), Trueperella pyogenes (9 cases, 2.7 per cent) and Listeria spp.(9 cases, 2.7 per cent)Leptospira spp. (8 cases, 2.4 per cent).The percentages of these isolates are very similar to those in 2017.

Toxoplasma gonddi continues to be the most commonly diagnosed pathogen despite vaccination being widely available. Susceptible sheep are infected following ingestion of food or water contaminated with oocysts, which are highly resistant and can survive for long periods in moist conditions. Infection in early gestation can result in foetal death and resorption, whilst infection late in gestation can result in lambs being born normal and immune. However, infection mid gestation usually result in still born lambs, weak lambs and mummified foetuses. Toxoplasma gondii is usually isolated from typical white lesions in placental cotyledons and foetal tissues (Figure 17.31). Ewes that abort due to Toxoplasma gondii, usually lamb normally in subsequent years.
